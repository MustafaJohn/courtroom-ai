Boogle Incorporated, Numbers Plc and MicroHard Ltd are very large IT companies that all produce software and databases, as well as some electronic consumer goods (which in turn require software to run). These companies are located in multiple locations in the UK and many Member States of the EU. 
Each company hires software engineers, programmers and other specialists at very high salaries.  These are the most significant costs of doing business for all these companies and each recognises the need to attract (and retain) exceptionally good talent. 
Up to six months ago, the three would actively attempt to “poach” talent from the other two. This “poaching” took the form of “cold-calls,” that is one firm calling the desired (“superstar”) employee of another firm and offering them a position with the calling firm.  The position would come with a greatly enhanced salary and set of benefits. To retain the superstar, the superstar’s own firm would have to offer to match or beat the competing offer.  This offer and counter-offer process could go on for a while, driving up the superstar’s wages (and thereby the costs for the “winning” firm).
Six months ago, the CEO of MicroHard gave an interview to an IT magazine (read by all working in the three companies) in which he stated that his company would no longer engage in the cold-calling process as it was costly not only for its competitors but also for the company itself. He also hoped that other companies “would see the advantages of his views.” Within two days of his interview the cold calls ceased altogether.
